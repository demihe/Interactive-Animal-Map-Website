Sacred Nation is a website dedicated to endangered species.

With the rapid growth of industries and the advancedment of technology,
many of Earth's precious species are endangered.

Endangered Species

    • X

      Ocelot

      - Leopardus Pardalis -

      The diet includes a variety of small mammals, birds and reptiles, as well as fish and also land crabs. Larger prey, such as agoutis, armadillos, monkeys, peccaries and deer are also an important part of the ocelot diet.

      The major threat to the ocelot is now habitat loss, with forest clearance for cattle ranching and agriculture reducing the natural cover on which the ocelot depends.

    • X

      Chacoan Peccary

      - Catagonus Wagneri -

      The Chacoan peccary was not recognised as a living species until 1975, making it one of the most recently discovered large mammals.

      The recent decline in the numbers of this species is due to hunting for meat, habitat destruction, and predation by larger animals like jaguars.

    • X

      Galapagos Land Iguana

      - Conolophus subcristatus -

      This iguana lives in the drier areas of the islands, in scrubby habitats. Females require access to areas of sandy or loose soil in which to lay their eggs

      Introduced animals are the main threat facing this species today

    • X

      Galapagos Penguin

      - Spheniscus Mendiculus -

      The Galapagos penguin is the smallest of the South American penguins, with an average length of less than 50 centimeters.

      The main threat facing this unique penguin is the increasing frequency of ENSO events, which reduce the strength of the cool currents this species needs to survive and causes fluctuations in its prey

    • X

      Giant Otter

      - Pteronura brasiliensis -

      Known through much of its range as the 'river wolf', the giant otter is the largest of the otter species in terms of total length. The diet is composed almost exclusively of fish

      Habitat loss and fragmentation, and pollution are currently the major threats to the survival of the giant otter, with the areas in which they live being destroyed and degraded by mining, logging, and damming

    • X

      Golden Poison Arrow Frog

      - Phyllobates terribilis -

      Golden poison frogs have a variety of bright vibrant colors that cover their entire bodies, from mint green to yellow to orange and sometimes white.

      Golden poison frog populations have been decreasing due to deforestation for agricultural purposes.

    • X

      Lears Macaw

      - Anodorhynchus Leari -

      Macaws can eat around 350 nuts in one day, using their massive beak to crack open the hard shells.

      Lear's macaw is thought to be a naturally rare species, and population numbers were low when it was discovered in 1978.

    • X

      Short Haired Chinchilla

      - Chinchilla Chinchilla -

      Short-tailed chinchillas make their dens in rock crevices. Chinchillas will eat any vegetation, such as grasses and herbs, within their range.

      The short-tailed chinchilla species is endangered due to habitat loss and hunting for its fine fur.

    • X

      South American Manatee

      - Trichechus inunguis -

      Amazonian manatees live almost entirely underwater. They feed entirely on aquatic vegetation near lake edges. Shy and secretive, only their nostrils protrude from the surface of the water to breathe as they search for lush vegetation.

      The Amazonian manatee has suffered from extensive hunting by subsistence and commercial hunters. It has been sought for meat, oil and fat, and at one time for its hide.

    • X

      Wandering Albatross

      - Diomedea exulans -

      Has the largest recorded wingspan of any bird, reaching up to 3.5 meters across. Albatrosses have tubular nostrils on either side of their upper bill instead of the more common fused nostrils of other bird species

      Longline fishing methods have made these creatures endangered.

    • X

      Swan Goose

      - Anser Cygnoides -

      The swan goose feeds at night, and the diet changes with the seasons.

      Habitat loss, particularly at breeding sites, is one of the major causes of the decline in populations of the swan goose.

    • X

      Russian Desman

      - Desmana moschata -

      Desmans belong to the same family as moles, but he tail is longer and flatter in order to adapt their aquatic lifestyle.

      Russian Desmans were taken for their fur and musk glands in the late 19th and early 20th century. Their habitat is getting smaller and more species are being introduced.

    • X

      Oriental Stork

      - Ciconia Boyciana -

      Oriental white stork will take fish, frogs, invertebrates, insects, voles, snakes, and even the chicks of other species to feed their own newborn chicks.

      Oriental Stork’s breeding site is being drained for agricultural development.

    • X

      Komodo Dragon

      - Varanus Komodoensis -

      The Komodo Dragon is the largest reptile in the world. It feeds mainly off of live prey such as deer, buffalo, and wild pigs and none of the meat goes to waste.

      The big reasons for this animal’s danger is that it is losing prey and habitat loss.

    • X

      Blakiston's Fish-owl

      - Ketupa Blakistoni -

      Blakiston’s fish owl is a specialised feeder on aquatic prey such as fish, amphibians and even small mammals.

      Habitat loss is the major cause of the decline in Blakiston’s fish owl numbers that has been documented throughout its range

    • X

      Arabian Ornoyx

      - Oryx Leucoryx -

      These antelope graze on grasses and herbs and will also take roots and tubers; they can go without direct water sources for long periods of time.

      The total decimation of the species did not occur until after the Second World War, with the availability of firearms and motorised transport, and the demand for sport hunting.

    • X

      Gharial

      - Gavialis Gangeticus -

      The gharial is one of the largest crocodilians with the narrowest snout of any species. Although adults feed primarily on fish, juvenile gharials sustain themselves on a vast array of invertebrates.

      The gharial came incredibly close to extinction in the 1970s, but a long-term captive breeding and reintroduction program was instrumental in improving the species status in the wild over the following decades.

    • X

      Giant Panda

      - Ailuropoda Melanoleuca -

      This species is unusual in having a ‘thumb’, which is actually a modified wrist bone that enables the giant panda to dextrously grasp bamboo stalks

      Habitat loss is the greatest cause of the decline of the giant panda.

    • X

      Mariana Flying Fox

      - Pteropus Mariannus -

      It is found in areas of native tropical forest, coastal strand, and mangroves.

      Continuing decline in the extent and quality of its habitat

    • X

      Red Crowned Crane

      - Grus japonensis -

      The red-crowned crane is sacred and seen as a symbol of fidelity, good luck, love and long life in the Orient.

      These beautiful birds were almost hunted to extinction in Japan at the beginning of the 20th century for their stunning plumage. Habitat losses due to agriculture and development have been further causes of the decline.

    • X

      Siberian Muskdeer

      - Moschus moschiferus -

      As the name suggests, musk deer are responsible for the production of musk, a strong-smelling substance that is one of the most expensive animal products in the world.

      The main threat to the Siberian musk deer comes from being hunted for the musk trade.

    • X

      Sumatran Tiger

      - Panthera Tigris Sumatrae -

      Accelerating deforestation and rampant poaching are the main reasons of the sumatran tiger’s decrease.

      Sumatran tigers are the smallest surviving tiger subspecies and are distinguished by heavy black stripes on their orange coats.

    • X

      Wild Bactrian Camel

      - Camelus Ferus -

      Previously thought to be the ancestor of all domestic camels, recent genetic studies have shown that the wild Bactrian camel is in fact a distinct species.

      Wild Bactrian camels have been heavily hunted for their meat and hide over the centuries.

    • X

      Dhole

      - Cuon Alpinus -

      Dholes are pack-living canids and are unique among this family in having a thick muzzle and one less molar tooth on each side of the lower jaw.

      Dhole numbers have been reduced as their habitat is being destroyed throughout much of the Asian continent.

    • X

      Great Bustard

      - Otis tarda -

      Some great bustard populations are migratory, and gather in large numbers at pre-migratory sites in order to move collectively to winter grounds.

      Mechanisation, chemical fertilisers and pesticides, fire and predation are serious threats for chicks and juveniles, and hunting of adults contributes to high mortality in some of their range countries.

    • X

      Axolotl

      - Ambystoma Mexicanum -

      These creatures can only be found in Mexico's lake complex of Xochimilco. The Axolotl Salamander can reach up to 30 centimeters and are usually black or albino. They can live up to 15 years and usually survive off of mollusks, insect larvae, fish, and worms.

      The Axolotl Salamanders are endangered because of lake contaminations. They are treated as a popular delicacy in Mexico.

    • X

      Beluga

      - Delphinapterus leucas -

      Beluga whales live in pods, or in groups of up to 25 that travel and hunt together. Belugas are very social and love to play and wrestle with one another.

      They are endangered because they are hunted by humans for their caviar.

    • X

      Giant Kangaroo Rat

      - Dipodomys ingens -

      Kangaroo Rats live in underground burrows and eat seeds and grains. They hop along the plains on their agile hind legs and use their long tails for balance. On average, they are about seven inches long and can live up to nine years.

      Kangaroo Rats are endangered because much of their natural desert habitat has been transformed into farmlands and oil fields.

    • X

      Giant Sea Bass

      - Stereolepis gigas -

      Sea bass start off bright orange with black spots then go on to become bronze-colored and eventually solid black.

      The black sea bass has been massively over-fished in both California and Mexican waters.

    • X

      Hammerhead Shark

      - Sphyrna mokarran -

      The hammerhead is usually found close inshore, particularly around reefs, and prefers warm temperate water.

      The hammerhead is targeted both directly and also taken as bycatch. It is prized for its fins for shark fin soup, as well as for its liver oil for vitamins, its skin for leather, and its meat for fish meal.

    • X

      Hawaiian Goose

      - Branta sandvicensis -

      The Hawaiian Goose lives in the islands of Hawaii and they mostly eat leaves, greens, and fruits.

      Why they are endangered: Their numbers have been decreasing due to attacks from mongooses and cats. People have been hunting them since 1949 and at one point the population decreased to only 30 Hawaiian geese. However, the population has been increasing to over thousands by 2001.

    • X

      Mountain Tapir

      - Tapirus Pinchaque -

      The Mountain Tapir’s diet consists of the twigs, fruits, and leaves of the Andean forest and paramos. They enjoy swimming and have very developed hearing and sense of smell.

      The Mountain Tapir is endangered because of habitat loss and warfare. Farming and agriculture has taken much of the Tapir’s land.

    • X

      Oahu Tree Snails

      - Achatinella -

      They are nocturnal, spending most of the day in the trees and can live their entire lives dwelling on one tree where they eat fungus that grows on the leaves.

      Oahu Tree Snails are endangered because of human settlement, over collecting of shells, and habitat destruction.

    • X

      Pygmy Raccoon

      - Procyon pygmaeus -

      The pygmy raccoon is the smallest of the raccoon species and found only on Cozumel Island, off the coast of Mexico, it is one of the most endangered carnivores in the world.

      Pygmy Raccoons are endangered by restriction to just one island, introduction of species, rapid tourist development, and hurricanes

    • X

      Volcano Rabbit

      - Romerolagus Diazi -

      The Volcano Rabbit is one of the smallest rabbits on the planet. These rabbits live in burrows and runaways around the volcano of Mexico City.

      These rabbits are going extinct because they are eaten by predators and are shot and trapped by farmers.

    • X

      Wood Bison

      - Bison bison athabascae -

      The American bison, the largest mammal in North America, once roamed the continent in vast herds and helped to shape the ecology of the Great Plains, as well as the history of the United States of America.

      Overhunting during the westward expansion of European settlement in the 19th century resulted in the decimation of the bison population

    • X

      California Condor

      - Gymnogyps californianus -

      The California condor is a vulture species that used to live along the entire Pacific coast of North America. Now it is only found in southern California.

      Threats to the California Condor include loss of habitat, shootings, pesticide residue, lead poisoning, and collisions with power lines.

    • X

      Franklin’s Bumblebee

      - Bombus franklini -

      Known only from southern Oregon and northern California between the Coast and Sierra-Cascade Ranges, with the most restricted range of any bumble bee in the world.

      Endangered because of exotic diseases, habitat loss due to destruction, degradation, conversion, and pesticides and pollution.

    • X

      Red-cockaded Woodpecker

      - Picoides borealis -

      Commonly found in the pine forests of the southeastern coastal plain, these birds require mature pine forests for their habitat, and the pines must be at least 80 years old. Diet consists mainly of insects such as beetles, ants, roaches, and caterpillars. They also eat eggs, larvae and spiders.

      The Red-cockaded Woodpecker suffers from habitat loss.

    • X

      Red Wolf

      - Canis rufus -

      Red wolves once lived in many habitats, such as swamps, forests, wetlands, and bushlands. Their diet consists of rodents, deer, and other small mammals like raccoons and rabbits.

      They were nearly wiped out because of human population growth, hunting, and the clearing of forest, eliminating much of its habitat. Red wolves preyed upon livestock, and many were killed by farmers and ranchers.

    • X

      White-headed Duck

      - Oxyura leucocephala -

      During the winter the white-headed duck forms large flocks which feed together on insect larvae and submerged plant material and seeds.

      The main threat to the Spanish population is its interbreeding with non-native North American ruddy ducks. Some young ruddy ducks escaped from captivity in 1952 and began to breed in the wild. The hybrids produced are fertile, and therefore pose an increasing threat to the white-headed duck.

    • X

      North Pacific Right Whale

      - Eubalaena japonica -

      The right whales, genus Eubalaena, obtained their common name from early whalers, as they were considered the ‘right’ whales to hunt.

      There are rare records of entanglements in fishing gear in the western North Pacific, but unlike in the North Atlantic there is currently no known mortality from ship collisions.

    • X

      European Rabbit

      - Oryctolagus cuniculus -

      Rabbits tend to be active during the evening and night, but in areas where they are undisturbed by humans they become more active during the day

      In 1953 the Myxoma virus killed a massive 99% of the British rabbit population

    • X

      Wild Goat

      - Capra aegagrus -

      The wild goat inhabits mountainous areas, where there is a mixture of rocky outcrops and vegetation

      Listed as vulnerable because of a population decline, estimated to be more than 30% over the last three generations, inferred from over-exploitation, shrinkage in distribution, and habitat destruction and degradation.

    • X

      Chinese Water Deer

      - Hydropotes inermis -

      The Chinese water deer lacks antlers, but the male has distinctive tusks.

      The Chinese water deer is in serious decline as a result of poaching and habitat destruction

    • X

      Asiatic Black Bear

      - Ursus Thibetanus -

      This Asiatic bear has a similar appearance to its better-known American relative with a stocky body, round head and large ears.

      The Asiatic black bear has been hunted for centuries for its skin, paws and for the gallbladder, which is used in Oriental medicine

    • X

      Caspian Seal

      - Pusa Caspica -

      One of the smallest of all seals, the Caspian seal is unusual in being confined to the inland, entirely landlocked Caspian Sea, the largest enclosed body of water on earth

      The main threat to the Caspian seal is intensive commercial hunting.

    • X

      Common Tortoise

      - Testudo Graeca -

      The Greek tortoise is also known as the spur-thighed tortoise for the large conical tubercle it has on each thigh.

      The Greek tortoise is threatened by illegal harvesting for the pet trade, as well as habitat loss and degradation.

    • X

      El Hierro Giant Lizard

      - Gallotia Simonyi -

      The Hierro giant lizard is a stocky reptile with a broad head and pronounced jowls.

      Feral rats and cats pose the most serious threat to this lizard. Poisons used for pest control seem to be inadvertently affecting the lizard population.

    • X

      European Bison

      - Bison Bonasus-

      European bison are the largest native herbivores in Europe and their appearance is very similar to North American bison except that they are smaller in stature and have a longer mane on the forehead.

      Threats to European bison include habitat fragmentation due to agricultural activity, logging, and unlimited hunting and poaching.

    • X

      European Ground Squirrel

      - Spermophilus Citellus -

      Although the European ground squirrel lives in burrows in loosely structured populations, it is not an exceptionally social or territorial species

      The conversion of steppe grassland and pasture into cultivated farmland and forestry is chiefly responsible for a serious decline in the population of European ground squirrels.

    • X

      European Mink

      - Mustela Lutreola -

      The European mink is one of Europe’s most endangered mammals. It is a medium sized mustelid with a long, slender, arched body, short legs and a short bushy tail.

      Habitat loss and degradation are serious threats to this sensitive species in parts of Europe, where hydroelectric developments and water pollution have increased significantly over the past few decades.

    • X

      Iberian Lynx

      - Lynx Pardinus -

      The Iberian lynx is the world's most threatened species of cat, and is currently teetering on the brink of extinction. They are generally nocturnal creatures, with peak activity occurring at twilight when individuals leave shelter in order to forage.

      Numbers of Iberian lynx have been decimated by habitat loss with human development such as dams, highways and railways all encroaching on its native habitat.

    • X

      Proteus

      - Proteus Anguinus -

      The cave salamander’s skin lacks pigment, giving its body a white, pasty appearance.

      The cave salamander is threatened by tourism, economic changes and industrial pollution as the caves are affected by the land-use above.

    • X

      Spanish Eagle

      - Aquila adalberti -

      This large eagle has a pale cream head and neck, but dark brown feathers elsewhere.

      The reason for this species being endangered include habitat fragmentation due to deforestation for agricultural land and timber has disturbed breeding grounds, intentional poisoning on hunting reserves to reduce natural predators of game species, lead shot poisoning as a result of ingestion of game killed with bullets, and electrocution on power cables.

    • X

      Takahe

      - Porphyrio hochstetteri -

      This unique flightless bird is roughly the size of a hen, making it the world's largest rail

      Habitat modification and the introduction of predators such as dogs to lowland habitats were significant in the decline of takahe numbers, although these birds were probably never particularly numerous

      • X

        Quokka

        - Setonix brachyurus -

        The quokka is a small marsupial similar in appearance to a wallaby or kangaroo, with distinctive short brown coarse hair and lighter underparts.

        There is real now concern for the population on Rottnest Island because the island is being developed for recreational purposes, resulting in habitat loss, and an increased spread of diseases from humans.

      • X

        Narwhal

        - Monodon monoceros -

        The narwhal's famous tusk is a hugely elongated tooth, and the largest ever recorded was over 2.5 meters long.

        Hunted by the Inuit as a subsistence food, narwhals are also hunted for their ivory tusks which are sold as curios or to be carved

      • X

        Leaf-scaled Sea Snake

        - Aipysurus foliosquama -

        With reddish-brown to purplish skin, male and female leaf-scaled sea snakes are similar in appearance, although the female is usually larger than the male.

        The threats to this species are largely unknown, but it is not thought to be affected by fisheries, as it is not a target species and is not subject to being accidentally caught in trawls, a fate which befalls many other sea snake species.

      • X

        Indo-pacific Humpback Dolphin

        - Sousa chinensis -

        Though usually grey, Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins can also be pink or white, a famous example being the ‘pink dolphins’ of Hong Kong bay.

        Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins are at risk from factors such as entanglement in fishing nets, pollution and the depletion of fish stocks worldwide

      • X

        Blue Bird of Paradise

        - Paradisaea rudolphi -

        The blue bird of paradise is endemic to Papua New Guinea. Like other birds of paradise, the blue bird of paradise performs an elaborate courtship display.

        Habitat loss is the most significant threat to the blue bird of paradise.

      • X

        Dugong

        - Dugong Dugon -

        The dugong is the only entirely marine mammal to feed exclusively on plants, a trait that leads to its other common name of 'sea cow'.

        The dugong has been traditionally persecuted by humans throughout much of its range for its meat, hide and oil.

      • X

        Goodfellow's Tree Kangaroo

        - Dendrolagus Goodfellowi -

        Like other tree kangaroos, Goodfellow’s tree kangaroo differs noticeably from the better-known ground kangaroos because it has developed specialised adaptations to its life in trees.

        This kangaroo is endangered as a result of over-hunting and habitat loss.

      • X

        Great Spotted Kiwi

        - Apteryx Haastii -

        The great spotted kiwi is a flightless bird with tough, leathery skin and feathers like hair. It is the national symbol of New Zealand.

        The Great Spotted Kiwi is in danger because of habitat loss, predation of chicks and juveniles by mustelids, nest predation by the brush-tailed possum, and predation of adults by dogs.

      • X

        Kowari

        - Dasyuroides byrnei -

        The kowari is a robust, rat-like carnivorous marsupial, with a pointed snout and a distinctive black brush on the terminal half of the tail, which may have a signalling function.

        Kowari is endangered by clearance of vegetation due to agriculture and overgrazing by cattle, rabbits and goats, which reduces cover and food for the kowari’s prey.

      • X

        Long-beaked Echidna

        - Zaglossus Bruijnii -

        Long-beaked echidnas are egg-laying mammals, known as monotremes, a group that also includes the duck-billed platypus and the short-beaked echidna.

        The decline of these echidnas is probably due mainly to hunting, which continues today with trained dogs.

      • X

        New Zealand Sea Lion

        - Phocarctos hookeri -

        The Hooker’s Sea Lion, also known as the New Zealand Sea Lion, is one of the rarest and most threatened sea lions in the world.

        Currently, the most serious current threat is accidental by-catch in the nets of the squid fishing industry.

      • X

        Tasmanian Devil

        - Sarcophilus harrisii -

        Haunted by its chilling nocturnal screeches, it was the early European settlers that gave the Tasmanian devil its alarming name.

        Since its discovery, Devil Facial Tumour Disease has had a colossal impact on the Tasmanian devil population, with overall sightings of devils on annual spotlight surveys declining by 80 percent. Untreatable, highly infectious, and consistently fatal, DFTD presents by far the biggest threat to the species’ survival.

      • X

        Victoria Crowned Pigeon

        - Goura Victoria -

        Crowned-pigeons are the largest pigeons in the world, immediately distinguished by the spectacular crest of lacy feathers on their heads.

        These beautiful birds have suffered rapid population declines as a result of habitat destruction and hunting.

      African Wild Dog

      - Lycaon Pictus -

      These long-legged canines have only four toes per foot, unlike other dogs, which have five toes on their forefeet.

      They are faced with shrinking room to roam in their African home. They are also quite susceptible to diseases spread by domestic animals.

    • X

      Black Rhinoceros

      - Diceros bicornis -

      Both black and white rhinoceroses are actually gray. They are different not in color but in lip shape. The black rhino has a pointed upper lip, while its white relative has a squared lip.

      Nearing extinction, the black rhinoceros has long been hunted for its horns, thought, in some cultures, to possess magical and medicinal qualities.

    • X

      Cheetah

      - Acinonyx Jubatus -

      Cheetahs are slim and have relatively long legs in relation to their body size. They have a carnivorous diet, of which a large portion includes gazelles.

      Genetic studies of cheetahs have shown that there is very little genetic variation within the species, possibly due to a severe bottleneck event during its evolutionary history. This leaves it extremely vulnerable to disease.

    • X

      Curvier's Gazelle

      - Gazella cuvieri -

      The hair is rather long, rough and coarse. The general colour of the coat is dull fawn, darker than that of the Dorcas Gazelle. Face markings are distinct.

      The major threats to the species are overhunting and habitat degradation, mainly due to the transformation of habitat into cropland and pastures for livestock, and for charcoal

    • X

      Fossa

      - Cryptoprocta ferox -

      The fossa is the largest carnivore and top predator native to Madagascar and is known to feed on lemurs and most other creatures it can get its claws on.

      Fossas are endangered creatures due to habitat loss.

    • X

      Grevy's Zebra

      - Equus grevyi -

      The largest of the living zebra species, Grevy's zebra can be distinguished from the other zebras by its larger ears and narrower stripes.

      While the species is no longer commercially hunted for its skin, numbers are continuing to decline due to competition with pastoral people and their domestic livestock, and to the long-term effects of overgrazing.

    • X

      Hippopotomus

      - Hippopotamus amphibius -

      Hippos spend up to 16 hours a day submerged in rivers and lakes to keep their massive bodies cool under the hot African sun. Hippos eat only grass and other plants.

      Hippos once had a broader distribution but now live in eastern central and southern sub-Saharan Africa, where their populations are in decline.

    • X

      Mountain Gorilla

      - Gorilla Beringei -

      The mountain gorilla has longer hair, jaws and teeth than the lowland subspecies. They eat the roots, leaves, stems, and pith of herbs, vines, shrubs, and bamboo.

      Gorilla species are endangered from poaching for body parts and for young animals collected for zoos and private collections, generating illegal income.

    • X

      Ruffed Lemur

      - Varecia Variegata -

      Black and white ruffed lemurs are among the largest of the true lemurs. It feeds on leaves, seeds and nectar according to the season.

      The main threats to the survival of ruffed lemurs are habitat destruction, hunting for meat and fur, and exportation.

    • X

      African Elephant

      - Loxodonta -

      The African Elephant is one of two elephants to remain from a formerly diverse evolutionary radiation. This group has thought to have originated in Africa during the Eocene.

      Overexploitation of elephants for their ivory has been a major threat to this shrinking species.

    • X

      Broad-striped Mongoose

      - Galidictis fasciata -

      Broad-striped mongooses are found only in the eastern rainforests of Madagascar. They feed mainly on rodents, small lemurs, and even reptiles and amphibians.

      It is endangered by an extremely limited range, which is threatened by clearance for maize cultivation and livestock grazing.

    • X

      Glass’s Shrew

      - Crocidura glassi -

      This species is known from east of the Great Rift Valley in the Ethiopian highlands where is occurs in isolated patches.

      The habitat of this restricted range species is threatened by fires and overgrazing by livestock.

    • X

      Hairy-eared Dwarf Lemur

      - Allocebus trichotis -

      This species is one of the rarest of the surviving lemurs. Until its rediscovery in 1989, it was known from only five museum specimens.

      It is endangered by habitat destruction resulting from slash-and-burn agriculture, being caught in traps, and being eaten by local people

    • X

      Shoebill

      - Balaeniceps rex -

      The prehistoric shoebill is one of the most impressive species in all of Africa. It is a predominantly solitary creature, coming together with another of its kind only to mate.

      It is endangered by habitat destruction and degradation, nest disturbance, increased hunting levels and capture for the bird trade.
